## 🚀 Featured Project

### 📦 The Hidden Cost of Delays — Analysis of 96,461 Olist orders (2016–2018)

> *Most analyses of the Olist dataset calculate the correlation between days of delay and ratings — and that’s as far as they go. This project goes deeper: it identifies **where, when and for whom** delays really take their toll.*

**Stack:** Python · Pandas · DuckDB / SQL · Scipy · Matplotlib · Seaborn · Power BI

| Analytical Question | What sets it apart |
|---|---|
| Is the delay symmetrical? | Analysis by product category and order value |
| Is there a tipping point in the rating? | Threshold on the 6th day, no linear correlation |
| Geography as a structural factor | Slow logistics vs. unrealistic delivery promises |
| The seller as a hidden variable | Variance within the same logistics corridor |

**Key findings:**
- There is a **threshold on the 6th day** of delay — from that point onwards, the rating plummets from ~3.7 to ~1.8 and stabilises. Reducing the delay from 8 to 6 days does not solve the problem; reducing it from 6 to 5 days can make all the difference.
- The `premium` tier (>R$1,048) achieves an 87% rating of 1 for critical delays — the most extreme result of the analysis.
- The North-East faces a double disadvantage: long delivery times and a disproportionately tight safety margin (ratio 0.62× vs. 1.08× in the South-East).
- On the MG→RJ corridor, the difference between the best and worst seller is **48 days** in `avg_delta` — validated by the Kruskal-Wallis test (H=163.26, p<0.0001).
